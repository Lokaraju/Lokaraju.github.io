Data Splitting in Machine Learning: Before or After Feature Engineering?

  • Jan 15, 2023
  • Machine Learning

When it comes to building robust machine learning models, one of the crucial steps is dividing the data into training and testing sets. This step allows us to evaluate the model's performance on unseen data and ensure that it generalizes well. However, the question often arises: Should we split the data before or after conducting exploratory data analysis (EDA), feature engineering, and feature selection? In this blog post, we'll explore the advantages and disadvantages of each approach and provide insights into real-time scenarios.

1. Data Splitting Before EDA and Feature Engineering

In this approach, we first split the original dataset into training and testing sets and then perform exploratory data analysis, feature engineering, and feature selection on the training data.

Advantages:

Data Integrity: By conducting EDA and feature engineering on the training data, we ensure that no information from the testing set is used to make decisions. This maintains the integrity of the evaluation process.

Simulation of Real-World Scenario: In real-world scenarios, we often encounter new, unseen data without any prior knowledge. By performing data preprocessing and feature engineering on the training set only, we mimic this real-world scenario more closely.

Disadvantages:

Risk of Data Leakage: If we're not careful, there's a risk of unintentional data leakage, where knowledge from the testing set influences the feature engineering process on the training set, leading to overly optimistic results during model evaluation. Limited Insights: EDA might be limited by the size of the training set, and we might miss potential patterns or insights that could be revealed by analyzing the entire dataset.

Data Splitting After Feature Engineering and Selection

In this approach, we perform EDA, feature engineering, and feature selection on the entire dataset, and then split it into training and testing sets.

Advantages:

Comprehensive Feature Engineering: By using the entire dataset for feature engineering, we can extract more robust and representative features, potentially leading to better model performance.

Rich Insights: Analyzing the complete dataset during EDA might reveal valuable insights that can inform feature engineering choices and model selection.

Disadvantages:

Risk of Overfitting: If we're not cautious, we might overfit our feature engineering choices to the entire dataset, leading to poor generalization on unseen data. Reduced Realism: This approach may not reflect the true real-world scenario where we don't have access to all the data during feature engineering.

Conclusion: Finding the Right Balance

In practice, the best approach often depends on the size of the dataset, the complexity of the problem, and the goals of the project. A balance between the two approaches is a common strategy:

1. Initial Data Split: Start with an initial data split before conducting extensive EDA and feature engineering. This helps prevent data leakage and ensures a realistic simulation of model evaluation.

2. Feature Engineering and Selection: Perform feature engineering and selection on the training set, focusing on generalization and model improvement.

3. Final Data Split: After finalizing the feature engineering and selection, perform a final data split, including cross-validation, to thoroughly evaluate the model's performance.

By following this hybrid approach, we can benefit from the advantages of both strategies while minimizing the associated risks.

In summary, the decision of when to split the data in a machine learning project requires careful consideration. Striking the right balance between maintaining data integrity and maximizing feature engineering benefits is key to building reliable and robust models that perform well on unseen data.
